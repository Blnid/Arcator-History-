The beginning of Arcator

Nov 2010 as a friend group
There were just three friends—Fela, Maren, and Jma. Three teens from London. They wanted to make an MC server back when Minecraft was still in Alpha. In November, it started—a time mostly cloaked in mystery now, with hardly any videos to remember it by. The server was meant to be a creative one, at a time when most of Minecraft was survival. People were given commands to /give because gamemodes didn’t yet exist. And despite that, they began to build.
Dec 2010 became publicly
As December came, new people joined by word of mouth. Many built homes, some made pixel art, and even a giant city was constructed—suspended in the sky. Despite players not being able to fly, they called it SkyCity, one of the largest builds. As December came to a close, Minecraft Beta was approaching. A new player joined by the name Kieraan they offered to help as an admin, while Llanoc a vetarin member by that time became one of the first moderators. The spawn was prepared as someting big was about to happen, the friends counted down, and just like that, the server took flight. It had grown from a small friend group into a public server. From just three friends, Arcator had begun.

The down fall of Arcator

—2011—
The server grew rapidly, with a dozen new people joining each day. The player count surged, and soon, every day, there were at least ten others online. The chat buzzed with activity, while some players stayed on IRC, watching the conversations unfold—the old kind of Discord. From 2011 to 2012, more than 3,000 people joined from all over the world. This was the golden age. The community was thriving, filled with creativity, friendships, and endless builds. But slowly, in 2012, cracks began to show. The first set of staff started to resign—first one, then several more, week by week. The once-solid foundation of leadership was beginning to weaken. Maren, the original owner, started preparing for succession and chose a new owner named Banana. Jma had disappeared, and Fela was beginning to move on as well. By mid-2012, Arcator had technically peaked, and the decline had begun. Fall 2012 was a time of many events, but most were never completed. The server, once updated regularly, began to fall behind. It became harder to discover and a hassle to join, as newer Minecraft versions outpaced it. 2013 was a slow burn. People still hoped for a revival, shared ideas, and waited. In a last attempt to save it, Banana rebooted Arcator—not as a creative server, but as a factions server. But before he could finish, interest had already faded. The last players finally left. The website, once full of activity, became a place of scattered posts. The server, once thriving, now lay dormant. Arcator had started as a massive creative server and ended as a small SMP.
